The job nobody demos

Put the vision model in your
pipeline, not your product.

A 450M-parameter VLM is too small to be the star of a feature. That is exactly why it is the right tool for the unglamorous job: understanding your entire image corpus — captioning, tagging, detecting, counting, triaging — on hardware you already own, over data that never leaves your network.

Every figure on this page is sourced to a primary document. Where this model is the wrong choice, this page says so. You are an engineer; you will check.

You already priced the three options. None of them fit the corpus.

Somewhere in your org sit 5–500 million images — claims photos, product shots, inspection frames, scanned media, app screenshots. Most are unlabeled, under-tagged, or described by a schema you have already outgrown. The shortlist:

Frontier API

Gemini Flash, GPT-4o-mini, Claude Haiku. Trivial to call — but you meter every image, you cannot send regulated data off-prem, and the per-image token bill is wildly uneven between vendors (more below). The killer: re-running the corpus means paying for all of it again.

Self-host 3–7B

Qwen2.5-VL-3B/7B. Genuinely capable, and Apache-licensed at 7B. But it is heavy per image; your batch throughput is GPU-bound and finance feels every node. The 3B is also Qwen-Research-licensed, not Apache — read the fine print.

Silicon on-device

A model tuned for a phone feature on a partner's NPU. Built to make one handset interaction feel instant — not to grind a data lake at batch. Frozen to the silicon roadmap, not yours.

The reframe

A product reads each image once. A pipeline re-reads the whole corpus — forever.

Every time the model improves, the taxonomy shifts, or a new downstream consumer shows up, a data pipeline re-processes the back catalog. That re-processing is where per-image pricing quietly becomes the largest line item in your ML budget — and where a model with ≈ $0 marginal cost stops being a discount and starts changing the architecture.

Once "look at this image" is free and local, you stop sampling and start processing everything, re-running on every schema change, enriching aggressively, and pointing it at data the API was never allowed to see.

You do not need this model to be brilliant. You need it:

  • Cheap enough to run over everything, repeatedly
  • Good enough to triage, tag, detect, and route
  • Small enough to fine-tune to your taxonomy
  • Private enough to point at data the API can't touch

At 450M parameters, LFM2.5-VL is all four. The interactive below is the whole argument in one number.

Why a 450M model is the right tool

Good enough to triage. Built for detection, description, counting — not transcription.

LFM2.5-VL-450M (released April 2026) pairs a 350M LFM2.5 hybrid-conv backbone with an 86M SigLIP2 vision encoder, handles images at native resolution up to 512×512, and added bounding-box grounding and function-calling in this generation. Against the obvious same-class peer, SmolVLM2-500M, it wins on every pipeline-relevant benchmark:

Where it is NOT the answer — so you don't get burned in week one

Dense text extraction. OCRBench 684 trails Qwen2.5-VL-7B's 864 (both 0–1000). If a pipeline stage's job is reading paragraphs off documents or invoices, route those frames to an OCR specialist or a larger model. This model earns its seat on the triage layer — detect, describe, tag, count, route — not the transcription layer. Pitching it as an OCR engine is how you lose the eval.

The spine · own a specialist, don't rent a generalist

Small enough to fine-tune in an afternoon, then it's yours — frozen, private, everywhere.

At 450M, you can fully fine-tune on a single GPU in hours — Liquid's own model card explicitly recommends fine-tuning for narrow use cases. So you don't bend your pipeline around a generalist's quirks; you bend a specialist to your taxonomy, pin the weights, and run the exact same artifact across your whole batch fleet.

No silent version bumps under you. No rate-limit tickets. No per-call meter. No model you're forbidden to ship into an air-gapped or on-prem environment. The model decision is one you control — which, not coincidentally, is the slot Liquid is built to win.

  • Yours: weights you hold, version you freeze — reproducible labels across re-runs
  • Private: point it at PHI/PII/regulated imagery; nothing leaves the boundary
  • Portable: Transformers, vLLM, SGLang, llama.cpp, MLX, ONNX, LEAP — CPU to Jetson to phone
  • Proven on-device: shipping embedded in Mercedes-Benz MB.OS in-car intelligence (2026)

Integration risk · removed

Pointing it at images is five lines.

# CPU or GPU, fully offline — same path that runs in your batch workers
brew install llama.cpp
hf download LiquidAI/LFM2.5-VL-450M-GGUF
llama-mtmd-cli -m LFM2.5-VL-450M-Q4_0.gguf --mmproj mmproj-LFM2.5-VL-450M-Q8_0.gguf \
  --image frame.jpg -p "List objects, their boxes, and a one-line caption as JSON."
# drop -ngl for pure CPU · swap in your fine-tuned weights · wrap in vLLM/SGLang for batch throughput

Day-zero support in Transformers ≥5.2, vLLM 0.19, SGLang, llama.cpp, MLX, ONNX Runtime, and the LEAP Edge SDK (iOS/Android/desktop/Linux/Windows). GGUF + mmproj is the recommended path; ExecuTorch coexists.

The ask · falsifiable in two weeks

A test designed to kill the idea fast if it's wrong.

  1. Take 100k images from one real corpus you already struggle to keep labeled.
  2. Fine-tune LFM2.5-VL-450M on your taxonomy on a single GPU (hours, not weeks).
  3. Run it locally over the set; capture tags, boxes, captions, triage routing.
  4. Measure triage precision/recall and cost-per-million against your current API or human-labeling baseline.

If it misses your bar, you've spent a GPU-week and learned it. If it clears it, you've removed a re-processing line item that compounds every quarter and a data-governance blocker — in the same move.
